Took three of my kids to a No Kings march today. It was one of those afternoons that felt genuinely good to be part of — big crowd, lots of energy, people of all ages who'd clearly put real thought and craft into what they wanted to say.

The signs were *fantastic*. Witty, angry, sincere, absurd, beautifully hand-lettered. At one point I counted three separate people who had independently landed on the same pun and were delighted to find their twins in the crowd.

And here's where I ran into a recurring, mildly comedic problem.

## The Sign Photography Problem

I wanted to document the signs. Genuinely! Some of them were works of art — the kind of thing you'd want to show people later and say *"look at this, someone made this today."* So I'd raise my phone, frame the shot, and then immediately confront the geometry of the situation:

The signs were being held by people. People with faces. People who had not consented to being in my camera roll. And — this being a protest with a strong demographic skew — a significant number of them were women, which meant that any attempt to photograph a sign at chest or shoulder height carried the very obvious visual implication that I was *not*, in fact, photographing the sign.

Reader, I put my phone away a lot.

The few shots I did take required a kind of elaborate staging: waiting for the sign to be raised above the crowd, finding a gap where the holder was at a distance, or doing the slightly awkward thing of making eye contact first and doing the universal *"can I photograph your sign?"* mime. Most people were immediately gracious about it. A few seemed genuinely pleased that someone wanted a picture.

But the ones I didn't ask — those signs are just living in my memory now, which is maybe how it should be.

## The Kids

My three were excellent march companions. One read every sign aloud as we passed it, providing a running commentary that was equal parts earnest and funny. One made friends with a dog wearing a small handmade banner. One mostly wanted to know when we were getting food, which is a completely valid political position.

At some point someone started a chant and the crowd picked it up and we were all just... yelling the same thing together in the afternoon sun, which is a strange and specific kind of collective joy that I don't think has a better name than *being at a protest*.

We got food after. It was a good day.
